## Abstract 
This article argues that static websites constitute a sustainable and versatile infrastructure for the dissemination and experimentation of digital history research. Drawing on the experience of the Luxembourg Centre for Contemporary and Digital History (C²DH), we detail how static web tools and workflows, such as AstroJS, GitHub Pages and Jekyll, can effectively empower research practices in creating, sharing, and iterating digital outputs. Static websites, we argue, do not only reduce the complexity of the technological infrastructure. They also enable more transparent workflows, greater involvement of less tech-savvy people, and long-term accessibility of results.

The article contends that these practices exemplify a model of co-creation between developers, designers, and historians, in which infrastructural design and scholarly production are closely intertwined. This collaborative model generates added value in both the production and dissemination of research outputs, and aligns with key principles of open science.

## Context: C²DH, DRI and the notion of co-creation
The [Luxembourg Centre for Contemporary and Digital History](https://www.uni.lu/c2dh-en/), hereafter C²DH, is one of five interdisciplinary centres at the University of Luxembourg, with a mission to establish a trading zone (Galison, 2010) for research in digital and contemporary history. Within this framework, its [Digital Research Infrastructure](https://www.uni.lu/c2dh-en/research-groups/digital-research-infrastructure/), hereafter DRI, plays a central role as a shared environment that facilitates the circulation of methods, tools, and expertise across projects and research areas. Rather than relying on generic, one-size-fits-all solutions, the DRI develops and maintains infrastructural components tailored to the specific epistemic and practical requirements of historical research (Kemman, 2021).

## Ranke.2

Ranke.2 is a scholar-led editorial project that publishes open-access lessons on digital source criticism specifically designed for historians. The project was initiated in 2014 by Fickers and Scagliola, launched in 2018 shortly after the creation of the Luxembourg Centre for Contemporary and Digital History. 

“Alternatives to mainstream publishing have different organisational forms and formats, but what unites them is being not-for-profit, not guided by quantity, and caring for thorough knowledge creation. These alternatives can be connected to universities or run by independent collectives, based in particular geographical locations or internationally, producing journals, books, blogs, translations or artistic work.” (the authors, 2021)

Ranke.2 was structurally conceived as a research, rather than editorial, project. Since its inception, it has been animated by a postdoctoral researcher, and a principal investigator, usually occupying the rank of associate professor, as the main tandem operating the project. Following the legal and administrative constraints, postdoctoral research contracts not being extensible beyond five years, the tandem is renewed following this periodicity. Thus, since the launch of the project in 2018,  and as by September 2024, two tandems succeeded to each other (Stefania Scagliola / Gerben Zaagsma then Sean Takats; and Sofia Papastamkou / Stefan Krebs). All researchers involved, as by the date this contribution is written, are historians. Additionally, the project team includes one designer (Daniele Guido) and one assistant developer (Frédéric Reis) who built the website and continuously offer technical support during the publication process or the technical maintenance of the website. The technical tandem ensures its support without discontinuity since the beginning. Furthermore, the project can periodically occupy student assistants, i.e. contractual student workers, who have contributed to editorial or technical tasks. Finally, Ranke.2 can lean on external collaborations for services such as translations of the lessons, copyediting or animation design.

Ranke.2 is built on open source infrastructure, its content is under [CC BY 4.0 license](https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/4.0/deed.en) and is open to the contributions of the community. 

Ranke.2 is published on a GitHub/jekyll based website. 
Ranke.2 runs on a static website hosted on the GitHub repository and built with the Jekyll generator (to be replaced in 2024 by Astro, following the deprecation of legacy configurations prior to Jekyll 4). In this, Ranke.2 follows the trend set by other editorial projects in digital humanities that “hacked” a system initially conceived to host developers code (GitHub) and used it to build scientific publications. The volunteer-led team of Programming Historian had been at the forefront back in 2014 to adopt a GitHub-based solution because of the advantages it offered for quick, and relatively effortless (for digital humanists at least), content publishing and maintenance, management via the same interface of publishing and peer review workflows, possibility for asynchronous collaborative work, and versioning of the revisions.
